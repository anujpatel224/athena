1936: An art deco masterpiece

Originally designed in 1936 by renowned architects Robert Arthur Bullivant and Harry Weedon, the Odeon Cinema opened its doors on 28th July 1938 at the junction of Rutland Street and Queen Street in Leicester. The inaugural screening featured Edward G. Robinson in A Slight Case of Murder.Boasting a striking art deco design, the venue seated 2,182 guests—1,307 in the stalls and 875 in the circle—making it one of the region's most extravagant and largest buildings of its time. Its architectural grandeur and cinematic offerings established the Odeon as a cultural landmark, setting the stage for its 59-year legacy as part of the prestigious Odeon cinema chain.

1960: A New Era of Entertainment

In the 1960s, the cinema was subdivided into four screens to cater to evolving viewing habits, marking a shift in how audiences experienced films. Despite these changes, the venue retained its grandeur and architectural beauty, remaining a cherished landmark.During this time, the cinema offered the latest films from the Rank Organisation while also embracing a variety of cultural events. Its shallow stage behind the screen hosted one-night performances by pop groups and singers, adding live music to its repertoire. Additionally, special screenings of opera and ballet films brought a touch of high culture to Leicester’s audiences, further cementing its status as a hub for entertainment and the arts.

1974: Adapting to Changing Times

As television gained popularity and posed challenges to traditional cinemas, the Odeon responded by embracing a new model of smaller-capacity screens. The grand single-auditorium layout was reimagined, splitting the venue into three mini cinemas. The circle and front stalls merged to create a larger screen seating 1,237 guests, while two smaller screens, seating 111 and 123 respectively, were built under the circle in the rear stalls. This innovation marked a turning point in the cinema's evolution, offering a variety of films within a single venue.

1990: A Period of Transition

By the 1990s, the venue had closed its doors, remaining dormant and awaiting its next chapter. The Odeon operated at the Queen Street site until July 16, 1997, when it relocated to a new 12-screen multiplex at Freemans Common, on the outskirts of Leicester city center. The final films screened at the Queen Street cinema were Batman and Robin, One Fine Day, Kama Sutra, and The Fifth Element. In 1998, the building was officially recognized for its architectural and cultural significance, earning Grade II listed status.

2021: Restoring an East Midland’s Icon

Amid the global pandemic, Athena Events Venue temporarily closed its doors but remained committed to adapting to the evolving needs of the event industry. Upon reopening, the team refocused on ensuring a safe and unforgettable event experience, implementing advanced technology to host events, offering flexibility to clients during uncertain times.

2024: Turn on the lights…. Again!

In 2024, Athena Events Venue introduced cutting-edge technological upgrades, including a state-of-the-art PA system, significantly enhancing its audio-visual capabilities. These improvements elevated the overall guest experience, solidifying Athena's reputation as one of Leicester’s premier event venues. The venue attracted both national and international clients, reaffirming its status as a top choice for events of all kinds.
